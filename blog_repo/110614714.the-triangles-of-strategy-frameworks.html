In the last blog, I pointed out some of the practical components of your security program. Still, it is essential to note that security programs are driven by the businesses they support. Understanding the business Vision, Mission, and Values is critical to developing your security program. Working with business leaders to understand their goals and objectives and how the security program can help drive results to their strategic initiatives.

Once there is alignment with the business on the Vision, Mission, and Values, it is time to create the approach for your team. Having a vision and strategic plan on how you will be servicing and supporting the business is a critical first step before budgeting your team. While your team is part of the business, you also want to ensure that your team has its own identity as a service partner to the company. During the early phases of building companies, some founders often focus on building compliance with the frameworks that help sell to their potential clients and operate in the regulatory environments in which they might work. Compliance might be a start to an approach. However, it should never be viewed as a high watermark but rather as a baseline. The triangle below is the Oversight & Compliance for the US Federal government and an excellent high-level overview of an approach that can be taken to understand the strategy and services that can be used in developing your new security team.

Compliance is just the start of your security management design. While I will be referencing a System Security Engineering — Compatibility Maturity Model, it does not have to be the only model you consider. I recommend this model because it allows for a continuous improvement path that can be used at the program level down to the level of the controls.

The above triangle can be looked at in a stepped approach as well. Demonstrated below shows how things can mature from ad-hoc to structured to managed & focused and eventually to continuous improvement.
